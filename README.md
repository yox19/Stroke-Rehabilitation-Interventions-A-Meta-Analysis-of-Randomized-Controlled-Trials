# Stroke-Rehabilitation-Interventions-A-Meta-Analysis-of-Randomized-Controlled-Trials

# Abstract

Stroke rehabilitation remains a major global challenge, particularly in low-resource settings where 
access to specialized centers is limited.[1] This meta-analysis evaluated three neuroplasticity-based 
interventions—mirror therapy (MRT), transcranial direct-current stimulation (tDCS), and virtualreality (VR) rehabilitation—to determine their relative effectiveness and feasibility for integration into 
routine hospital care.

# Methods

A PRISMA-guided search across PubMed, Medline, Google Scholar, the Cochrane Library, and Web 
of Science (2015–2025) identified 142 randomized controlled trials (RCTs).[2] After removing 
duplicates and studies not meeting inclusion criteria (target intervention, population, or outcome; 
full-text availability; no confounding interventions), 12 high-quality studies with 489 participants 
were included. Eligible studies compared MR, tDCS, or VR versus conventional rehabilitation (CRT) 
in adults with ischemic or hemorrhagic stroke, measuring upper limb recovery via the Fugl-Meyer 
Assessment (FMA). Figure 1
Meta-analysis was conducted using fixed-effect inverse-variance model in RevMan 5.4.1. 
Heterogeneity was assessed via Cochrane Q and I². SPSS 27 was used for supplementary descriptive 
and regression analysis.

# Results

All three interventions showed statistically significant upper-limb motor recovery compared with 
conventional physiotherapy. Figure 2 Figure 3 Figure 4
• tDCS vs CRT: (MD) +7.4 FMA points (95% CI: 4.5–10.2; Z=6.61; p<0.00001; I²=54%).

• VR vs CRT: MD +5.5 points (95% CI: 2.9–8.1; Z=4.12; p<0.00001; I²=0%).

• MRT vs CRT: MD +6.5 points (95% CI: 4.5–8.6; Z=6.22; p<0.0001; I²=0%).

Mirror therapy achieved the most favorable balance between effect size (d = 1.4) and feasibility, 
requiring minimal equipment and training. Sensitivity analysis (removing large-sample studies) 
showed that overall effect estimates for tDCS and VR were somewhat sensitive to sample size, while 
results for mirror therapy were more robust.
